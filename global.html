How personal experiences of community care inspired a global perspective on shared challenges

My POV

Introduction

My worldview was shaped long before I knew what a worldview was. Both sides of family are based in Pucallpa, Peru—a small city in the Amazon that most people have never heard of. Growing up, I'd spend summers there with my family, and coming back to Raleigh each fall felt like switching between two completely different worlds. I'm incredibly proud of being so connected to my culture, and knowing Spanish isn't just a skill—it's literally how I talk to my grandparents, how I understand who I am.

Having the opportunity to visit Peru as much as I did growing up gave me something invaluable: the deep understanding that there are countless ways to live, to solve problems, and to build community. Knowing Spanish isn't just a skill for me—it's my connection to my roots, to my family, and to a whole way of seeing the world that I carry with me everywhere I go.

High school is when a lot of things clicked for me. It's when I realized how interested I was in political science and environmental science, when I really started to appreciate how incredible it is to know two languages (inspiring me to learn a third), and when I made the connection between how what I do in my life locally does indeed play a part in the rest of the world. When my community in Raleigh lifted me up during those challenging years, I was simultaneously learning about the Amazon being deforested near where my family lives. I started to see that we have to have this collective thinking to move forward and care for each other.

This realization that everything is connected—that caring about your immediate community and caring about the world are the same thing—drove me to keep exploring. Being deeply rooted in my Peruvian identity while growing up in North Carolina sparked a curiosity about the world that has driven every major decision I've made. I genuinely love the feeling of being somewhere completely new, of figuring out unfamiliar systems, and of discovering the unique ways different communities approach shared challenges. It's why I've studied abroad three times—Cuba, Prague, and Paris—and why I'm always planning my next adventure. =

Learning French has been one of my most rewarding challenges. There's something magical about feeling your brain rewire as you acquire a new language, about those moments when a conversation suddenly clicks and you realize you're actually thinking in French. Combined with my Spanish fluency from my Peruvian heritage, these languages have opened doors not just to new places, but to new ways of understanding how people communicate, connect, and see the world.

I seek to understand because I know I'm small in this big world, and the solutions we need require all of our voices—especially the ones that haven't been heard yet.
